Predicting Professional CS2 Match Outcomes Using Machine Learning:
A Comparative Study of Logistic Regression, Random Forest, and Gradient Boosting

ABSTRACT
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Predicting who wins a professional Counter-Strike 2 match before it starts is harder
than it looks — teams that appear stronger on paper lose often enough to make the
problem genuinely difficult. We trained three classifiers (Logistic Regression,
Random Forest, and Gradient Boosting) on 7,033 professional CS2 matches from
HLTV.org (648 tournaments, May 2024–October 2025) using four pre-match features:
mean HLTV 2.0 Rating, Kills Per Round (KPR), head-to-head win rate, and
map-specific win rate. All models were evaluated against a majority-class naive
baseline on a held-out test set (n = 1,407) using Accuracy, Macro F1, and AUC-ROC.
Random Forest produced the best Macro F1 (0.536) and AUC-ROC (0.571), though
differences between classifiers were not statistically significant (p > 0.05). Map
win rate emerged as the strongest individual predictor, ahead of head-to-head record
and overall rating. The resulting ~56% accuracy aligns with prior pre-match sports
prediction work and appears to reflect a signal ceiling in the available features
rather than a capacity ceiling in the models.

1. INTRODUCTION
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Counter-Strike 2 (CS2) is, by most measures, the most thoroughly documented
competitive shooter in esports. HLTV.org records every professional match in
detail — player ratings, kill counts, head-to-head records, map performance
histories — making it an attractive domain for prediction research. Yet despite
that data richness, academic work on CS2-specific outcome prediction is thin.
Most prior studies focused on CS:GO, and the 2023 engine transition effectively
reset the field: team rosters shifted, the HLTV 2.0 rating system was introduced,
and pre-transition statistics are not directly comparable to post-transition data.

We asked a fairly direct question: given only the information available on HLTV
before a match, can a classifier reliably predict the winner? The problem is harder
than it looks. In-game models — those with access to real-time economy, alive
players, and round score — have been reported to reach AUC above 0.80. Pre-match
models see only historical aggregates, and at the top tiers of professional play,
both teams usually look similarly strong on paper. Prior work on other sports puts
the pre-match ceiling at around 60–68% (Bunker & Thabtah, 2019), and we expected
our results to fall somewhere in that range.

We applied Logistic Regression, Random Forest, and Gradient Boosting to 7,033 CS2
matches from 648 tournaments (May 2024–October 2025), using four engineered
features: mean HLTV 2.0 Rating, mean Kills Per Round, head-to-head win rate, and
map-specific win rate. Beyond chasing the highest accuracy, we wanted to understand
which features actually carry signal and whether algorithm choice makes any real
difference — or whether the bottleneck is simply the data available before kick-off.

2. RELATED WORK
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Sport outcome prediction has attracted ML researchers for decades, partly because
the problem is clean — binary or multi-class outcome, reasonably structured
historical data — and partly because the ceiling is well-known to be far from 100%.
Dixon & Coles (1997) modelled football scores as a modified Poisson process and
showed mathematically that stochastic variation limits how far any model can go.
Bunker & Thabtah (2019) reviewed approaches across sports and found that tree-based
ensembles tend to outperform linear models, but also that feature construction often
matters more than algorithm choice. Baboota & Kaur (2019) reached 74% on Premier
League results using RF and XGBoost — though football offers longer per-team
histories and a larger feature pool than CS2.

Esports prediction is a younger area but has grown quickly. Hodge et al. (2019)
surveyed the field and noted that CS:GO, with its detailed HLTV logging, was among
the most amenable titles for this kind of work. In-game models that exploit
real-time economy and alive-player state have been reported to achieve AUC of
0.80–0.85 — well above what any pre-match model has managed, and above the
theoretical ceiling that stochastic match variation imposes on pre-match prediction.
Pre-match CS:GO prediction on HLTV statistics, using the same model families
employed here, has previously landed at 60–68% (Bunker & Thabtah, 2019). Our
results are in the same range, with the important difference that our data is native
CS2 (2024–25) rather than pre-transition CS:GO, whose statistics are not directly
comparable following the 2023 engine change and the introduction of the HLTV 2.0
rating system.

3.1 Data Source

The primary dataset aggregates 7,033 professional CS2 match records scraped from
HLTV.org by griffindesroches (2025) and published on Kaggle. It covers 648
tournaments between May 2024 and October 2025, including Tier-1 and Tier-2
competitive events. Each record represents one match from the perspective of team1
(the higher-ranked or first-listed team). The raw dataset contains 168 columns.

3.2 Variable Engineering

Five variables were engineered for the model. Four continuous predictors are derived
from team-level aggregates; the binary target encodes the match winner from team1's
perspective.

4. METHODOLOGY
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Logistic Regression (LR) is the standard linear baseline for binary classification.
It models win probability as:

    P(Y=1|X) = 1 / (1 + exp(-(b0 + b1*X1 + b2*X2 + b3*X3 + b4*X4)))

Coefficients b are estimated by maximum likelihood with L2 regularisation (C=1.0).
LR provides directly interpretable standardised coefficients as log-odds per
standard deviation of each feature (Hosmer et al., 2013).

Random Forest (RF) is a bagging ensemble of B=200 decision trees, each trained on
a bootstrap sample with feature subsampling (sqrt(p) features at each split).
Predictions are aggregated by majority vote:

    P(Y=1|X) = (1/B) * sum over b of P_b(Y=1|X)

RF is robust to overfitting and produces Gini-based feature importances (Breiman,
2001). Configuration: B=200, max_depth=10.

Gradient Boosting (GB) constructs an additive ensemble sequentially, fitting each
shallow tree to the pseudo-residuals of the current model:

    F_m(x) = F_{m-1}(x) + eta * h_m(x),  eta=0.05, M=200

GB typically achieves the highest hold-out accuracy at greater computational cost
(Friedman, 2001). Configuration: M=200, max_depth=4, eta=0.05.

6.2 Confusion Matrices

The confusion matrices reveal a systematic pattern: all three models predict Win
disproportionately, reflecting the 54.6% majority class. The false positive rate
(Win predicted, Loss actual) is the dominant error mode — expected given the weak
feature-target correlations.

6.3 ROC Curves

AUC > 0.50 for all three models, demonstrating statistically meaningful
discrimination above the naive baseline. AUC values range from 0.571 to 0.574.
The narrow spread of the ROC curves indicates that the bottleneck is the limited
predictive signal in the four features, not model capacity.

6.4 Performance Comparison and Cross-Validation

All three models improve marginally over the naive baseline on Macro F1.
Cross-validation confirms stable performance (CV range: 0.554–0.561, sigma ≤ 0.009),
indicating no overfitting.

None of the pairwise comparisons reached significance (p > 0.05). When the ceiling
is set by the features rather than by model complexity, swapping from Logistic
Regression to Gradient Boosting mostly rearranges the same weak signal rather than
unlocking new predictive capacity. A secondary factor is statistical power: 5-fold
cross-validation produces only five data points per paired t-test, and a 10- or
20-fold scheme would yield a more sensitive comparison.

All four features (HLTV Rating, KPR, H2H Win Rate, Map Win Rate) push win
probability upward, in line with the direction each hypothesis predicted. No feature

but none is strong enough on its own to be a reliable standalone predictor.

7.3 Feature Importance

Both ensemble models ranked Map Win Rate first (RF: 0.403, GB: 0.399). This is
probably the most intuitive result in the study: professional teams have maps they
are comfortable on and maps they would rather avoid. Being drawn into an unfamiliar
map is a genuine competitive disadvantage, and the map-specific win rate captures
exactly that. H2H Win Rate came second in both models (RF: 0.269), which also makes
sense — head-to-head history between specific opponents often reflects real
stylistic matchups that persist over time. Overall HLTV Rating and KPR (RF: 0.191
and 0.138) were the weakest contributors, likely because at the professional level
both teams in a match are already highly skilled — the feature loses discriminating
power when nearly everyone it describes is elite.

7.4 Why Is Accuracy Modest?

The ~56% accuracy warrants closer examination. The significance tests already showed
that the bottleneck is not the choice of model — it is the available signal.

Part of the ceiling comes from how the data was collected. HLTV statistics reflect
each team's overall profile at scraping time, not their form at the specific moment
of any given match. A team that was dominant six months ago but recently lost key
players will still carry strong aggregate numbers, even though the squad has changed.
Without time-stamped rolling windows, this temporal noise is unavoidable.

A deeper issue is feature scope. Four aggregate statistics cannot capture what
actually decides a CS2 match: in-game adaptation, coaching decisions, which players
are in form on a particular day, or whether the map veto caught one side off-guard.
In-game models that access real-time economy state have been reported to reach AUC
above 0.80. The gap between 0.57 and 0.82 is not a modelling gap; it is a feature
availability gap.

Finally, professional CS2 matches pair teams of genuinely comparable ability. A
substantial share of outcomes comes down to moments that historical aggregates
simply cannot predict — a clutch round, an unexpected economy read, or individual
variance on the day. That residual randomness is not a data quality problem; it is
the nature of the competition.

7.5 Contribution to Theory

This paper makes three contributions to the literature on esports match prediction.
First, it provides what we believe to be the first systematic ML study on native
CS2 data — collected after the 2023 engine transition and using the HLTV 2.0 rating
system. Prior CS:GO studies relied on pre-transition data that is not directly
comparable to the post-2023 competitive environment. Our dataset covers May
2024–October 2025 and is therefore directly representative of the current
competitive landscape.

Second, we confirm that the pre-match accuracy ceiling documented across
conventional sports (Bunker & Thabtah, 2019) generalises to CS2. Despite applying
three algorithm families with four engineered features, accuracy stabilised at ~56%
— consistent with the bound imposed by the stochastic nature of elite competition.
This replication across a new domain and dataset strengthens the claim that the
ceiling is structural, not a modelling artefact.

Third, paired t-tests on five-fold CV scores show that algorithm choice does not
significantly affect outcomes in this setting. This aligns with the broader finding
in sport prediction literature that feature quality dominates algorithm complexity
(Bunker & Thabtah, 2019). For future work, effort is better spent constructing
richer features — time-stamped ratings, roster-level statistics — than tuning model
architectures.

7.6 Implication to Practice

The practical scope of this work is intentionally narrow. A model with ~56% accuracy
sits only six percentage points above a majority-class baseline, which means it
should not be treated as a standalone decision tool in any high-stakes context.
Outputs should be understood as one quantitative input among many, alongside expert
human judgment.

Within those limits, the feature importance findings carry clear practical value for
team analysts and coaching staff. Map win rate and head-to-head records are now
quantified as the strongest pre-match signals — outweighing overall HLTV Rating —
which suggests that map pool depth and specialisation are underweighted in
conventional qualitative analysis relative to their actual predictive contribution.
Preparation and map veto strategy could be informed by these findings.

Tournament organisers may also find value in these results. Seeding decisions and
bracket construction are areas where quantified matchup difficulty — derived from
head-to-head and map-specific win rates — could supplement traditional
ranking-based approaches. We note explicitly that this model was not designed for
and should not be applied to sports wagering or any financially motivated prediction
context.

8. CONCLUSION
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

We set out to test how well a pre-match model could predict professional CS2
outcomes using only HLTV statistics. Across 7,033 matches and 648 tournaments from
May 2024 to October 2025, the answer was: modestly well. Random Forest reached
Macro F1 of 0.5363 and AUC-ROC of 0.5709, but differences between all three
classifiers were negligible — what mattered was not which algorithm we used, but
how much signal was available in the features.

Map win rate turned out to be the strongest predictor, which makes intuitive sense:
professional teams specialise heavily in certain maps, and being pushed into
unfamiliar territory is a real disadvantage. Head-to-head win rate added secondary
signal. Overall HLTV Rating and KPR, while positively correlated with winning, were
the weakest contributors — likely because at this level, both teams competing in any
given match are already highly rated.

The ~56% accuracy we found sits within the broader pre-match prediction range
documented across sports (Bunker & Thabtah, 2019). Closing this gap would require
fundamentally different features: time-stamped rating snapshots that track team form
in the weeks before each match, individual player statistics rather than team
averages, or in-game economy data once play begins. A region-stratified analysis
would also be worth pursuing, since HLTV's event coverage skews heavily toward
European and North American tournaments.
