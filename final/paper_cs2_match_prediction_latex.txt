\title{Predicting Professional CS2 Match Outcomes Using Machine Learning:\\
A Comparative Study of Logistic Regression, Random Forest, and Gradient Boosting}

\author{Seisenbek Dias}
\author{Mergen Temirzhan}
\author{Onashov Aidos}

\address{School of Digital Technology, Narxoz University, Almaty, Kazakhstan}

\begin{abstract}
Predicting who wins a professional Counter-Strike 2 match before it starts is harder
than it looks --- teams that appear stronger on paper lose often enough to make the
problem genuinely difficult. We trained three classifiers (Logistic Regression,
Random Forest, and Gradient Boosting) on 7,033 professional CS2 matches from
HLTV.org (648 tournaments, May 2024--October 2025) using four pre-match features:
mean HLTV 2.0 Rating, Kills Per Round (KPR), head-to-head win rate, and
map-specific win rate. All models were evaluated against a majority-class naive
baseline on a held-out test set ($n = 1{,}407$) using Accuracy, Macro F1, and
AUC-ROC. Random Forest produced the best Macro F1 (0.536) and AUC-ROC (0.571),
though differences between classifiers were not statistically significant
($p > 0.05$). Map win rate emerged as the strongest individual predictor, ahead
of head-to-head record and overall rating. The resulting ${\sim}56\%$ accuracy
aligns with prior pre-match sports prediction work and appears to reflect a signal
ceiling in the available features rather than a capacity ceiling in the models.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Counter-Strike 2 (CS2) is, by most measures, the most thoroughly documented
competitive shooter in esports. HLTV.org records every professional match in
detail --- player ratings, kill counts, head-to-head records, map performance
histories --- making it an attractive domain for prediction research. Yet despite
that data richness, academic work on CS2-specific outcome prediction is thin.
Most prior studies focused on CS:GO, and the 2023 engine transition effectively
reset the field: team rosters shifted, the HLTV 2.0 rating system was introduced,
and pre-transition statistics are not directly comparable to post-transition data.

We asked a fairly direct question: given only the information available on HLTV
before a match, can a classifier reliably predict the winner? The problem is harder
than it looks. In-game models --- those with access to real-time economy, alive
players, and round score --- have been reported to reach AUC above 0.80. Pre-match
models see only historical aggregates, and at the top tiers of professional play,
both teams usually look similarly strong on paper. Prior work on other sports puts
the pre-match ceiling at around 60--68\% \citep{Bunker2019}, and we
expected our results to fall somewhere in that range.

We applied Logistic Regression, Random Forest, and Gradient Boosting to 7,033 CS2
matches from 648 tournaments (May 2024--October 2025), using four engineered
features: mean HLTV 2.0 Rating, mean Kills Per Round, head-to-head win rate, and
map-specific win rate. Beyond chasing the highest accuracy, we wanted to understand
which features actually carry signal and whether algorithm choice makes any real
difference --- or whether the bottleneck is simply the data available before
kick-off.

\section{Related Work}

Sport outcome prediction has attracted ML researchers for decades, partly because
the problem is clean --- binary or multi-class outcome, reasonably structured
historical data --- and partly because the ceiling is well-known to be far from
100\%. \citet{Broms2024} applied Logistic Regression and Random Forest to CS:GO match
data from HLTV.org over a three-year period, benchmarking predictions against
bookmaker odds --- the closest prior study to our own.
\citet{Bunker2019} reviewed approaches across sports and found that tree-based
ensembles tend to outperform linear models, but also that feature construction often
matters more than algorithm choice. \citet{Baboota2019} reached 74\% on Premier
League results using RF and XGBoost --- though football offers longer per-team
histories and a larger feature pool than CS2.

Esports prediction is a younger area but has grown quickly. \citet{Hodge2019}
surveyed the field and noted that CS:GO, with its detailed HLTV logging, was among
the most amenable titles for this kind of work. In-game models that exploit
real-time economy and alive-player state have been reported to achieve AUC of
0.80--0.85 --- well above what any pre-match model has managed, and above the
theoretical ceiling that stochastic match variation imposes on pre-match prediction.
Pre-match CS:GO prediction on HLTV statistics, using the same model families
employed here, has previously landed at 60--68\% \citep{Bunker2019}. Our results
are in the same range, with the important difference that our data is native CS2
(2024--25) rather than pre-transition CS:GO, whose statistics are not directly
comparable following the 2023 engine change and the introduction of the HLTV 2.0
rating system.

\section{Dataset and Variables}

\subsection{Data Source}

The primary dataset aggregates 7,033 professional CS2 match records scraped from
HLTV.org by \citet{griffindesroches2025} and published on Kaggle. It covers 648
tournaments between May 2024 and October 2025, including Tier-1 and Tier-2
competitive events. Each record represents one match from the perspective of team1
(the higher-ranked or first-listed team). The raw dataset contains 168 columns.

\section{Methodology}

\textbf{Logistic Regression (LR)} is the standard linear baseline for binary
classification. It models win probability as:
\begin{equation}
P(Y=1|X) = \frac{1}{1 + \exp\!\bigl(-(\beta_0 + \beta_1 X_1 + \beta_2 X_2
            + \beta_3 X_3 + \beta_4 X_4)\bigr)}
\end{equation}
Coefficients $\beta$ are estimated by maximum likelihood with L2 regularisation
($C=1.0$). LR provides directly interpretable standardised coefficients as
log-odds per standard deviation of each feature \citep{Hosmer2013}.

\textbf{Random Forest (RF)} is a bagging ensemble of $B=200$ decision trees, each
trained on a bootstrap sample with feature subsampling ($\sqrt{p}$ features at each
split). Predictions are aggregated by majority vote:
\begin{equation}
P(Y=1|X) = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B} P_b(Y=1|X)
\end{equation}
RF is robust to overfitting and produces Gini-based feature importances
\citep{Breiman2001}. Configuration: $B=200$, max\_depth=10.

\textbf{Gradient Boosting (GB)} constructs an additive ensemble sequentially,
fitting each shallow tree to the pseudo-residuals of the current model:
\begin{equation}
F_m(x) = F_{m-1}(x) + \eta \cdot h_m(x), \quad \eta = 0.05,\; M = 200
\end{equation}
GB typically achieves the highest hold-out accuracy at greater computational cost
\citep{Friedman2001}. Configuration: $M=200$, max\_depth=4, $\eta=0.05$.

\section{Experimental Setup}

All experiments run in Python 3.11 using scikit-learn 1.x \citep{Pedregosa2011} with
\texttt{random\_state=42} throughout. Full code is available at
\url{https://github.com/aloneen/cs2-match-outcome-prediction}. Macro F1 is the
primary metric; AUC-ROC is secondary. The naive baseline (always predict Win) is
included in all comparisons. Statistical significance of model differences is
assessed by paired $t$-test on 5-fold CV accuracy ($\alpha=0.05$).

\subsection{Statistical Significance}

None of the pairwise comparisons reached significance ($p > 0.05$). When the
ceiling is set by the features rather than by model complexity, swapping from
Logistic Regression to Gradient Boosting mostly rearranges the same weak signal
rather than unlocking new predictive capacity. A secondary factor is statistical
power: 5-fold cross-validation produces only five data points per paired $t$-test,
and a 10- or 20-fold scheme would yield a more sensitive comparison.

\subsection{Feature Importance}

Both ensemble models ranked Map Win Rate first (RF: 0.403, GB: 0.399). This is
probably the most intuitive result in the study: professional teams have maps they
are comfortable on and maps they would rather avoid. Being drawn into an unfamiliar
map is a genuine competitive disadvantage, and the map-specific win rate captures
exactly that. H2H Win Rate came second in both models (RF: 0.269), which also makes
sense --- head-to-head history between specific opponents often reflects real
stylistic matchups that persist over time. Overall HLTV Rating and KPR
(RF: 0.191 and 0.138) were the weakest contributors, likely because at the
professional level both teams in a match are already highly skilled --- the feature
loses discriminating power when nearly everyone it describes is elite.

\subsection{Why Is Accuracy Modest?}

The ${\sim}56\%$ accuracy warrants closer examination. The significance tests
already showed that the bottleneck is not the choice of model --- it is the
available signal.

Part of the ceiling comes from how the data was collected. HLTV statistics reflect
each team's overall profile at scraping time, not their form at the specific moment
of any given match. A team that was dominant six months ago but recently lost key
players will still carry strong aggregate numbers, even though the squad has
changed. Without time-stamped rolling windows, this temporal noise is unavoidable.

A deeper issue is feature scope. Four aggregate statistics cannot capture what
actually decides a CS2 match: in-game adaptation, coaching decisions, which players
are in form on a particular day, or whether the map veto caught one side off-guard.
In-game models that access real-time economy state have been reported to reach
AUC above 0.80. The gap between 0.57 and 0.82 is not a modelling gap; it is a
feature availability gap.

Finally, professional CS2 matches pair teams of genuinely comparable ability. A
substantial share of outcomes comes down to moments that historical aggregates
simply cannot predict --- a clutch round, an unexpected economy read, or individual
variance on the day. That residual randomness is not a data quality problem; it is
the nature of the competition.

\subsection{Contribution to Theory}

This paper makes three contributions to the literature on esports match prediction.
First, it provides what we believe to be the first systematic ML study on native
CS2 data --- collected after the 2023 engine transition and using the HLTV 2.0
rating system. Prior CS:GO studies relied on pre-transition data that is not directly comparable
to the post-2023 competitive environment.
Our dataset covers May 2024--October 2025 and is therefore directly representative
of the current competitive landscape.

Second, we confirm that the pre-match accuracy ceiling documented across
conventional sports \citep{Bunker2019} generalises to CS2. Despite
applying three algorithm families with four engineered features, accuracy stabilised
at ${\sim}56\%$ --- consistent with the bound imposed by the stochastic nature of
elite competition. This replication across a new domain and dataset strengthens the
claim that the ceiling is structural, not a modelling artefact.

Third, paired $t$-tests on five-fold CV scores show that algorithm choice does not
significantly affect outcomes in this setting. This aligns with the broader finding
in sport prediction literature that feature quality dominates algorithm complexity
\citep{Bunker2019}. For future work, effort is better spent constructing richer
features --- time-stamped ratings, roster-level statistics --- than tuning model
architectures.

\subsection{Implication to Practice}

The practical scope of this work is intentionally narrow. A model with ${\sim}56\%$
accuracy sits only six percentage points above a majority-class baseline, which
means it should not be treated as a standalone decision tool in any high-stakes
context. Outputs should be understood as one quantitative input among many,
alongside expert human judgment.

Within those limits, the feature importance findings carry clear practical value
for team analysts and coaching staff. Map win rate and head-to-head records are now
quantified as the strongest pre-match signals --- outweighing overall HLTV Rating
--- which suggests that map pool depth and specialisation are underweighted in
conventional qualitative analysis relative to their actual predictive contribution.
Preparation and map veto strategy could be informed by these findings.

Tournament organisers may also find value in these results. Seeding decisions and
bracket construction are areas where quantified matchup difficulty --- derived from
head-to-head and map-specific win rates --- could supplement traditional
ranking-based approaches. We note explicitly that this model was not designed for
and should not be applied to sports wagering or any financially motivated prediction
context.

\section{Conclusion}

We set out to test how well a pre-match model could predict professional CS2
outcomes using only HLTV statistics. Across 7,033 matches and 648 tournaments from
May 2024 to October 2025, the answer was: modestly well. Random Forest reached
Macro F1 of 0.5363 and AUC-ROC of 0.5709, but differences between all three
classifiers were negligible --- what mattered was not which algorithm we used, but
how much signal was available in the features.

Map win rate turned out to be the strongest predictor, which makes intuitive sense:
professional teams specialise heavily in certain maps, and being pushed into
unfamiliar territory is a real disadvantage. Head-to-head win rate added secondary
signal. Overall HLTV Rating and KPR, while positively correlated with winning, were
the weakest contributors --- likely because at this level, both teams competing in
any given match are already highly rated.

The ${\sim}56\%$ accuracy we found sits within the broader pre-match prediction
range documented across sports \citep{Bunker2019}. Closing this gap would require fundamentally different
features: time-stamped rating snapshots that track team form in the weeks before
each match, individual player statistics rather than team averages, or in-game
economy data once play begins. A region-stratified analysis would also be worth
pursuing, since HLTV's event coverage skews heavily toward European and North
American tournaments.
